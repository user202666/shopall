What is THC Flower?

THC flower = the unprocessed flower or bud of the cannabis plant that naturally contains tetrahydrocannabinol (THC). It is smoked, vaped, or used in edibles for both medical and recreational purposes (in other countries). THC is what causes the "high" feeling, or delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol, is the primary psychoactive compound in cannabis. It exerts its effects by interacting with the body’s endocannabinoid system, particularly the CB1 receptors in the brain and central nervous system. THC is responsible for the “high” commonly associated with cannabis use, but it also has potential therapeutic applications. Medical research has focused extensively on understanding both the benefits and risks of THC in various clinical contexts, with increasing interest as legalization spreads and formulations become more standardized. Evidence supports the use of THC—sometimes in combination with CBD—for several medical conditions. Clinical trials and meta-analyses have shown that THC-based treatments can provide modest relief for chronic pain, particularly neuropathic pain, and may also help manage muscle spasticity in conditions like multiple sclerosis. In oncology, THC has demonstrated effectiveness in reducing nausea and vomiting caused by chemotherapy, leading to the approval of synthetic THC medications such as dronabinol and nabilone in some countries. Additionally, THC can stimulate appetite in patients suffering from HIV/AIDS-related cachexia or other wasting syndromes. Early trials and real-world data suggest some benefit for palliative care, where THC may reduce symptom burden and improve quality of life. There’s also growing interest in THC/CBD combinations for treatment-resistant epilepsy, particularly in children, although high-quality data remains limited. However, THC is not without significant risks, and many of these are dose- and age-dependent. Frequent use of high-THC cannabis has been associated with the development of Cannabis Use Disorder (CUD), a form of dependency. Research has also linked THC exposure—particularly during adolescence or in genetically vulnerable individuals—to an increased risk of psychotic symptoms, cognitive impairment, and mental health issues such as anxiety and depression. Short-term effects of THC include impaired attention, short-term memory loss, and altered judgment. These effects can persist or become chronic in some users, especially those who begin using at an early age. Smoking cannabis also presents respiratory risks, and there are concerns about cardiovascular effects such as increased heart rate and blood pressure, especially in older or at-risk individuals. Pregnant users may expose fetuses to developmental harm, including low birth weight and possible cognitive delays. Despite increasing acceptance of cannabis in medicine, there are still major gaps in our understanding of THC’s long-term effects and optimal usage. Questions remain about the best dosage, route of administration (smoking vs. vaporization vs. oral oils), and THC:CBD ratios to balance therapeutic effects with safety. It is also unclear whether CBD meaningfully reduces the adverse effects of THC, with some studies suggesting limited protective benefits. Furthermore, few large-scale, long-term randomized controlled trials exist, and many studies suffer from small sample sizes or inconsistent product formulations. Researchers are actively exploring the role of THC in managing conditions such as epilepsy, PTSD, anxiety disorders, cancer symptom burden, and even neurodegenerative diseases, but definitive conclusions have yet to be drawn.
